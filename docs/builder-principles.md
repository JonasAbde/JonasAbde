# AI Builder Principles

These are the principles I use when building AI-assisted tools and automation workflows.

## 1. Start with a real workflow

AI is most useful when it is connected to a real task or business process.

Before building, I try to understand:

- What information exists?
- Who uses it?
- What decision needs to be made?
- What action should happen next?
- What is currently manual or repetitive?

## 2. Keep the first version small

I prefer building a small prototype quickly instead of planning a large system too early.

A good first version should prove one thing:

> Can this workflow become easier, faster, or more useful with AI?

## 3. Structure before automation

Bad structure creates bad automation.

Before automating, I try to define:

- Inputs
- Outputs
- Rules
- Edge cases
- Human review points
- Failure modes

## 4. Human review for important actions

For business, customer, financial, or sensitive workflows, AI should support the human — not silently act without control.

I prefer systems where AI prepares, organizes, and suggests, while important actions can still be reviewed.

## 5. Useful beats flashy

The best AI tool is not always the most advanced one.

A simple workflow that saves time every week is more valuable than a complex demo that nobody uses.

## 6. Document what works

I try to document:

- What problem the prototype solves
- What tools were used
- What worked
- What failed
- What should be improved next

This makes projects easier to explain, improve, and reuse.

## 7. Build for daily operations

I am especially interested in AI systems that help with daily work:

- Planning
- Follow-up
- Customer communication
- Summaries
- Decisions
- Internal tools
- Business overview

The goal is practical impact, not AI for its own sake.
